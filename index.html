Introduction

I have a simple question for you. Have you ever thought, how we can find the area of a shape? We know to find the area of a rectangle, we can multiply the length and width. We know the area of a triangle is half of the base times the height. But what if we have a shape that is not a rectangle or a triangle, some weird shape?

Yeah, Yeah, I know, we can use Integration. But the question is, do you understand how integration works? Do you understand the fundamental of integration? If not, then this article is for you. In this article, we will learn about the concept of calculus (Integration / Differentiation) and how it works.

I want to find the circumference of a circle

The thing is, I don’t know how to find the circumference of a circle. I know the formula for the circumference of a circle is \(C = 2\pi r\), but I don’t know where it came from. So, let’s try to find the circumference of a circle without using the formula.

The good news!

The one good news is, we know how to find the perimeter of a square. Can we use that knowledge to find the circumference of a circle? Let’s try to find the circumference of a circle by approximating it with a square.

Not good enough? Let’s try to approximate it with a regular polygon. The more sides the regular polygon has, the closer it will be to a circle. So, let’s try to find the circumference of a circle by approximating it with a regular polygon.

NoteTip

You can click on the graph to access the Desmos calculator and play around with the number of sides of the regular polygon to see how it approaches the circumference of the circle.

OK, let’s say we have a regular polygon with \(N\) sides inscribed in a circle of radius \(r\). And we can draw a triangle from the center of the circle to two adjacent vertices of the polygon. Let the central angle be \(\Delta\theta\). Then the length of each side of the regular polygon is given by:

\[ \Delta s = 2 r\sin\left(\frac{\Delta\theta}{2}\right) \]

That’s sorted out! Now, we can find the perimeter of the regular polygon by summing up the lengths of all the sides:

\[ C_N=\sum_{k=1}^{N} \Delta s=N\Delta s \]

\[ C_N = N\Delta s \]

Yeah! we can just say \(C_N = N\Delta s\) because all the sides of the regular polygon are equal. But we can also write it as a summation, so we can generalize the theory later in the article.

NoteReference

If you confised about how I simplified the summation, you can check out this article:

Paul’s Online Math Notes – Summation Notation

Now, we can write the perimeter of the regular polygon as:

\[ C_N = 2r N \sin\left(\frac{\Delta\theta}{2}\right) \]

We clearly see that \(C_N\) is not the circumference of the circle, but we can see that as \(N\) increases, \(C_N\) approaches the circumference of the circle. So, shall we say that:

\[ C = \lim_{N \to \infty} C_N \]

\[ C = 2r \lim_{N \to \infty} N \sin\left(\frac{\Delta\theta}{2}\right) \]

This is interesting. Because we can see that \(2r\) is a constant for all values of \(N\). The only term that is changing is \(\lim_{N \to \infty} N \sin\left(\frac{\Delta\theta}{2}\right)\). So, we can focus on that term. Let’s call that term \(\beta_N\).

As \(N\) increases, the regular \(N\)-gon hugs the circle more closely, and \(\beta_N\) approaches a limit.

For many centuries, mathematicians tried to estimate this limit using polygon ideas like this. Archimedes’ method in the 3rd century BCE is the classical example.

Shall we try to find the area of a function?

Let’s say we have a function \(f(x)\), and we want to find the area under the curve of that function between \(x=a\) and \(x=b\). It is true that we don’t know the formula for the area. But the good news is, we know how to find the area of a rectangle.

Using the lesson we learned from the previous section, we can divide the area under the curve into rectangles. The more rectangles we use, the better the approximation. So, let’s try to find the area under the curve by approximating it with rectangles.

NoteTip

You can click on the graph to access the Desmos calculator and play around with the number divisions to see how it approaches the area under the curve. It has trepizodial rule as well, so you can play around with that as well.

Let’s say the interval \([a,b]\) is divided into \(N\) subintervals of equal width \(\Delta x = \frac{b-a}{N}\). Then, the area under the curve can be approximated by the sum of the areas of the rectangles:

\[\begin{aligned} A_N &= \Delta A_1 + \Delta A_2 + \ldots + \Delta A_N \\ &= f(a + \Delta x) \Delta x + f(a + 2\Delta x) \Delta x + \ldots + f(a + N\Delta x) \Delta x\\ &= \sum_{k=1}^{N} f(a + k\Delta x) \Delta x\\ &= \sum_{k=1}^{N} f\left(a + k \frac{b-a}{N}\right) \frac{b-a}{N} \end{aligned}\]

Finally, let’s make the width of the rectangles infinitesimally small by taking the limit as \(N\) approaches infinity:

\[ A = \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{k=1}^{N} f\left(a + k \frac{b-a}{N}\right) \frac{b-a}{N} \]

Let’s try an example

Let’s say we want to find the area under the curve of the function \(f(x) = -x^2 + 5x - 1\) between \(x=1\) and \(x=4\). Then, we can write the area as:

\[ A = \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{k=1}^{N} f\left(1 + k \frac{4-1}{N}\right) \frac{4-1}{N} = \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{k=1}^{N} f\left(1 + \frac{3k}{N}\right) \frac{3}{N} \]

Then, we can find \(f\left(1 + \frac{3k}{N}\right)\) and substitute it back into the summation:

\[\begin{aligned} f\left(1 + \frac{3k}{N}\right) &= -\left(1 + \frac{3k}{N}\right)^2 + 5\left(1 + \frac{3k}{N}\right) - 1 \\ &= -\left(1 + \frac{6k}{N} + \frac{9k^2}{N^2}\right) + 5\left(1 + \frac{3k}{N}\right) - 1 \\ &= -1 - \frac{6k}{N} - \frac{9k^2}{N^2} + 5 + \frac{15k}{N} - 1 \\ &= 3 + \frac{9k}{N} - \frac{9k^2}{N^2} \end{aligned}\]

\[ f\left(1 + \frac{3k}{N}\right) \frac{3}{N} = \left(3 + \frac{9k}{N} - \frac{9k^2}{N^2}\right) \frac{3}{N} = \frac{9}{N} + \frac{27k}{N^2} - \frac{27k^2}{N^3} \]

By substituting it back into the summation, we get: \[ A = \lim_{N \to \infty} \sum_{k=1}^{N} \left(\frac{9}{N} + \frac{27k}{N^2} - \frac{27k^2}{N^3}\right) \]

Now, we can split the summation into three separate summations: \[ A = \lim_{N \to \infty} \left( \sum_{k=1}^{N} \frac{9}{N} + \sum_{k=1}^{N} \frac{27k}{N^2} - \sum_{k=1}^{N} \frac{27k^2}{N^3} \right) \]

Now, we can simplify each summation using the formulas for the sum of the first \(N\) natural numbers and the sum of the squares of the first \(N\) natural numbers:

\[\begin{aligned} \sum_{k=1}^{N} \frac{9}{N} &= 9 \\ \sum_{k=1}^{N} \frac{27k}{N^2} &= \frac{27}{N^2} \sum_{k=1}^{N} k = \frac{27}{N^2} \cdot \frac{N(N+1)}{2} \\ &= \frac{27(N+1)}{2N} \\ \sum_{k=1}^{N} \frac{27k^2}{N^3} &= \frac{27}{N^3} \sum_{k=1}^{N} k^2 = \frac{27}{N^3} \cdot \frac{N(N+1)(2N+1)}{6} \\ &= \frac{9(N+1)(2N+1)}{2N^2} \end{aligned}\]

We can substitute these back into the expression for \(A\):

\[\begin{aligned} A &= \lim_{N \to \infty} \left( 9 + \frac{27(N+1)}{2N} - \frac{9(N+1)(2N+1)}{2N^2} \right) \\ &= \lim_{N \to \infty} \left( \frac{18N^2 + 27N^2 + 27N - (18N^2+27N+9)}{2N^2} \right) \\ &= \lim_{N \to \infty} \left( \frac{27}{2} - \frac{9}{2N^2} \right)\\ \end{aligned}\]

Finally, we can take the limit as \(N\) approaches infinity: \[ A = \frac{27}{2} \]

Well, that was easy enough. And this result is infact correct. You can verify it by using integration.

NoteNote

We used squares to approximate the area under the curve, but we can also use trapezoids to get a better approximation. The process is the same, we just need to use the formula for the area of a trapezoid instead of the area of a rectangle. Try it …

TipLesson learned

If we want to find a arbitary shape area, we can devide it to rectangles (or trapezoids), find the area of each rectangle, and sum them up. As we increase the number of rectangles (i.e making the rectangles infinitesimally small), the approximation gets better and better, and in the limit, we get the exact area of the shape.

Symbolizing the process

We learned that we can find the area under the curve of a function \(f(x)\) between \(x=a\) and \(x=b\) by using the summation, and taking the limit as the number of rectangles approaches infinity. This process is the basis of the definite integral. It is a good idea to give this a symbol, so we can use it to find the area under the curve of any function.

Let’s denote the area under the curve of a function \(f(x)\) between \(x=a\) and \(x=b\) as:

\[ \boxed{A=\lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \sum_{k=1}^{N} f\left(a + k \Delta x\right) \Delta x = \int_a^b f(x) dx} \]

Finally, Let’s define the indefinite integral as: \[ F(x) = \int_0^x f(t) dt \]

\[ \boxed{F(x) = \int f(t) dt + C} \]

Here, the first term (indefinite integral) tells us the area under the curve in the interval \([0,x]\), and the second term \(C\) (constant of integration) tells us about the interval \([-\infty, 0]\).

Does this process has a reverse?

Let’s write the \(F(x)\), temporely ignoring the limit,

\[ F(x) = \sum_{k=1}^{N} f\left(k \Delta x\right) \Delta x \]

\[ F(x+\Delta x) = \sum_{k=1}^{N+1} f\left(k \Delta x\right) \Delta x = \sum_{k=1}^{N} f\left(k \Delta x\right) \Delta x + f\left((N+1) \Delta x\right) \Delta x \]

\[ F(x+\Delta x) = F(x) + f\left((N+1) \Delta x\right) \Delta x \]

We can identify that \(f\left((N+1) \Delta x\right)\) is the value of the function at \(x+\Delta x\). So, we can write it as: \[ F(x+\Delta x) = F(x) + f\left(x+\Delta x\right) \Delta x \]

\[ \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{F(x+\Delta x) - F(x)}{\Delta x} = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} f\left(x+\Delta x\right) = f(x) \]

\[ \boxed{f(x) = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{F(x+\Delta x) - F(x)}{\Delta x} = \frac{dF(x)}{dx}} \]

Let’s call this process of finding the reverse of the integration as differentiation. We can also call it the derivative of \(F(x)\) with respect to \(x\).
